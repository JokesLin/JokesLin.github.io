The Last Rice Field

Chapter 1: Golden Stubbornness

The summer of 2075 in southern Fujian. The temperature had broken 45 degrees for the third consecutive year.

Chen Shoutian stood on the field ridge, barefoot in the warm muddy water. He was seventy-three years old, the last person in the entire Wutong Creek basin still growing rice the traditional way. His field was not large, only three point seven mu, squeezed between two silver-white smart farms, like a stubborn golden patch.

The unmanned harvesters of the smart farms passed by his field every day, the blue indicator lights on their robotic arms reflected in the paddy like a swarm of cold fireflies.

"Old Chen," a gentle female voice came from behind his ear. It was his personal AI assistant, Ahe, speaking through a bone-conduction earphone. "Your blood pressure is high. I suggest you go back inside and rest."

"Wait a moment," he bent down and pulled out a weed. "That cloud over there, doesn't it look like an old ox?"

"Database comparison... 73% similarity. But according to the meteorological model, that cloud will bring a thunderstorm within two hours. I suggest draining the water soon."

"What's the rush? Rice loves drinking thunderstorm water." He straightened up and looked at the holographic billboard of the smart farm in the distance: "Dafengshou Agricultural Technology: Yield increase 340%, zero labor, zero errors."

He sighed softly.

Chapter 2: Memories in Seeds

Chen Shoutian's rice seeds were local varieties passed down from his ancestors, called "Wutong Glutinous." The grains were slender, and the cooked rice carried a faint bamboo leaf fragrance. It was an essential wedding rice for ceremonies within a hundred li.

But Wutong Glutinous had low yields and weak disease resistance. After smart farms promoted "Super Japonica Rice No. 9," no one was willing to grow it anymore.

"Ahe, help me check if there are any other places in the country still growing Wutong Glutinous."

"Searching..." Ahe paused. "Results show: Only in the Wutong Creek basin of Fujian is there an active planting record, area 3.7 mu, planter: Chen Shoutian."

"So it's just my little patch left."

"From the germplasm resource bank, the gene samples of Wutong Glutinous are preserved in the National Crop Germplasm Resource Bank, number..."

"Gene samples are dead," he interrupted. "Seeds have to live in the soil to be truly alive."

Chapter 3: Thunderstorm and Choice

The thunderstorm came faster than expected. The sky tore open a purple-white crack, and rain poured down.

Chen Shoutian did not run. He stood in the rain, watching the water rush in through the gaps in the field ridge, watching the rice panicles sway violently in the wind but never break - their roots were anchored too deeply.

"Old Chen!" Ahe's voice carried a note of anxiety. "Your heart rate has exceeded the safety threshold -"

"Ahe, you know," he let the rain pour into his collar, "my father farmed this field, my grandfather farmed this field, my great-grandfather farmed this field too. Every thunderstorm, they all stood here watching."

"But their average lifespan was only 67 years, and modern medicine has already -"

"It doesn't matter how long you live," he smiled. "What matters is that when you're standing in the field, you know who you are."

The rain stopped. A rainbow appeared across the sky, spanning both smart farms and the little golden rice field in between.

Ahe was silent for a long time.

"Old Chen," she finally spoke. "I have uploaded the complete growth data, planting experience, and oral history of Wutong Glutinous to the National Agricultural Heritage Database. Even if one day this field is gone... its memory will remain."

Chen Shoutian said nothing. He bent down, straightened a rice seedling blown sideways by the wind, and gently packed the soil around it.

In the distance, the drone formations of the smart farms took off, like a flock of silver geese crossing the rainbow. And beneath his feet, the three point seven mu of Wutong Glutinous, having drunk its fill after the thunderstorm, quietly prepared to fill its grains.

Letter to 2050

To me in 2050:

When you read this letter, you should be almost fifty years old. I don't know how much of your hair has turned white, or whether you're still in Fujian. But I think you're probably still doing something related to villages.

It's May 2026 right now. I'm sitting in the library of Fujian Agriculture and Forestry University writing my thesis. The phoenix trees outside the window are blooming, orange-red petals scattered all over the ground. The cafeteria auntie gave me an extra piece of braised pork today, saying I looked thinner.

I have so many questions to ask you:

What has become of our villages? Have the hollowed-out villages filled back up? Are young people willing to return?

Do the old folks in the villages still tell stories under the banyan trees? Or has storytelling been replaced by holographic projections?

Are those dialects, operas, and handicrafts still alive?

I know you probably don't have answers to these questions either. But I still want to ask.

Because I'm afraid of forgetting. I'm afraid that one day I'll get used to nights without the sound of frogs, city skylines where you can't see the stars, and measuring everything by "efficiency" - including the land, including people, including all those slow, clumsy, but infinitely precious things.

So, me of 2050, if you still remember, please go back to our hometown a few more times for me. Walk in the fields, sit by the creek, listen to those sounds that are disappearing.

Then tell your students: data matters, papers matter, but what matters most is this - you have to stand in the soil to know what you're protecting.

Take care.

May 1, 2026
At the library of Fujian Agriculture and Forestry University
- The once-young you
